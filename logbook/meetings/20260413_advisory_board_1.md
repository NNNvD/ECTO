## Notes
- The advisory board's role is to provide documented input on major project decisions and to be available for consultation between the six-monthly meetings if issues arise.
- Public-facing project materials live in GitHub and are archived in Zenodo with DOI-backed versions; confidential expert data, consent forms, and personalized responses are stored in Yoda.
- Current recruitment status shared in the meeting: 5 acceptances, 5 declines, and 10 open invitations, with a broader pool of roughly 160 candidate names and a little over 40 identified automatically via ORCID.
- The current timeline is to draft onboarding materials and consent forms now, launch the first survey in June, run one survey round per month, allow 2 to 3 weeks for responses, and use about one week between rounds for synthesis and preparation.
- The project phases were described as: phase 1 on consensus about what theoretical psychologists should do, phase 2 on learning goals, and phase 3 on textbook content that addresses those learning goals.
- Board feedback on recruitment emphasized that direct calls can help when possible, explicit deadlines can reduce drift, fewer reminder cycles may speed replacement, and invitation emails should make the low workload and benefits more salient.
- The discussion also highlighted that women may be less likely to self-identify as experts, so explicit invitation language and a transparent selection rationale may help improve balance in the panel.

## Decisions
- The advisory board approved increasing the number of open expert invitations from 15 to 20.
- The advisory board supported inviting additional experts outside the standard identification procedure if the rationale is documented transparently.
- The advisory board was open to making participation visible to invitees rather than keeping participants anonymous if that improves recruitment.
- The project will keep the current eligibility threshold at assistant professor level and above for now rather than adding postdocs.

## Actions
- Draft onboarding materials, consent forms, and the first survey for a June launch.
- Increase the number of concurrent open expert invitations from 15 to 20.
- Shorten the reminder and replacement cycle by setting clearer response deadlines and inviting new experts sooner after non-response.
- Invite the additional experts outside the standard procedure and document why each was added.
- Decide whether participation should remain anonymous to invitees or whether participant names should be visible to support recruitment.
- Review the invitation email to make the workload, timeline, and benefits more salient.
