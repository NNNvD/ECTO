# Formal Dissent Protocol

## Purpose
The purpose of this protocol is to ensure that substantive disagreements are documented transparently when they cannot be fully resolved through revision and additional Delphi rounds. We distinguish between:
- **ordinary disagreement**, which is expected in Delphi rounds and is handled through revision and rescoring
- **formal dissent**, which is a persistent, reasoned objection that a participant wishes to have recorded in the project record and, where relevant, in public outputs

## Guiding principles
We adhere to the following principles:
- **Consensus is preferred, but not forced.**
- **Disagreement is informative, not merely problematic.**
- **Dissent is reasoned, concise, and relevant to the item under discussion.**
- **Dissent concerns content, interpretation, scope, method, pedagogy, procedure, or implications, not persons.**
- **A participant may dissent from a specific item without rejecting the project as a whole.**
- **Dissent does not automatically affect authorship or contributor status.**

## Definitions
### Ordinary disagreement
Ordinary disagreement occurs when a participant scores an item below the consensus threshold or provides critical comments, but does not request a formal record of unresolved objection.

### Formal dissent
Formal dissent is a structured statement indicating that, even after revision, a participant judges a proposed item, section, recommendation, or framing to be:
- conceptually misleading
- methodologically unsound
- too narrow, exclusionary, or unbalanced in scope
- pedagogically inappropriate
- inconsistent with the project’s stated aims or evidence base

### Procedural dissent
Procedural dissent is a structured objection not to the content itself, but to the process by which the item was handled, such as insufficient revision time, unclear wording of the vote, or improper application of decision rules.

## When a formal dissent may be filed
A formal dissent may be filed when:
- an item **meets the consensus threshold**, but a participant still has a substantial unresolved objection
- an item **fails to meet the threshold**, and the participant judges that the reasons for disagreement should be formally preserved
- a participant judges that a proposed resolution misrepresents the state of the evidence or the legitimate range of expert positions

A formal dissent is generally filed after round feedback is circulated and before the item is finalized.

## Submission procedure
A formal dissent is submitted in writing within **7 days** after round feedback is circulated.

Each dissent includes:
- **Item identifier**  
  Example: Phase 2, Item 4.3, “Chapter structure for model evaluation”
- **Type of dissent**  
  Conceptual / methodological / scope-related / pedagogical / procedural / ethical
- **Short title**  
  Example: “Concern about overly narrow definition of theory”
- **Dissent statement**  
  Maximum 250 words
- **Reasoning**  
  Brief explanation of why the item is problematic
- **Suggested remedy**  
  Revision, split into multiple items, additional round, minority note, or publication with dissent
- **Publication preference**  
  Signed, anonymized, or undecided until final output stage

## Screening and admissibility
The PI screens a dissent only for procedural admissibility, not for substantive agreement.

A dissent is admissible if it is:
- relevant to the item
- stated in professional language
- non-redundant or meaningfully distinct from previously submitted dissents
- specific enough to be addressed

A dissent may be returned for revision if it is:
- ad hominem
- too vague
- outside the scope of the item
- purely repetitive without adding a distinct rationale

A dissent is not rejected merely because it disagrees with the majority.

## How we handle dissents
After a formal dissent is submitted, we choose one of the following responses:

### 1. Revise and resubmit
We revise the item in light of the dissent and return it in the next round.

### 2. Split the item
If the disagreement reflects multiple underlying questions, we split the item into separate items.

### 3. Record as unresolved and proceed
We proceed with the item, but formally record the dissent because the disagreement is substantive and is not likely to disappear through minor revision.

### 4. Escalate to targeted discussion
If needed, we organize a short focused consultation among relevant experts before the next round.

## Publication rules

### 1. No consensus reached
If an item does not meet the consensus threshold after the planned number of rounds:
- it is marked as **no consensus**
- item-level agreement statistics are reported
- major reasons for disagreement are summarized
- formal dissents may be included in a supplement or appendix

### 2. Consensus reached, but formal dissent remains
If an item reaches the threshold, but one or more participants maintain a formal dissent:
- the item may still be included as a **consensus position**
- the final output notes that **consensus was reached with recorded dissent**
- the dissent appears in one of the following places:
  - appendix
  - chapter note
  - boxed minority view
  - online supplement

### 3. Dissent has broad pedagogical importance
If the disagreement reflects an enduring divide in the field that is educationally important, we present both the consensus position and the dissent in the main text.

## Format for published dissent notes
A published dissent note uses the following format:
- **Item**
- **Nature of dissent**
- **Dissent statement** (maximum 250 words)
- **Optional editorial response** (maximum 150 words)

## Anonymity and attribution
We use the following attribution procedure:
- **During Delphi rounds:** dissent remains anonymous to other panelists
- **At final publication stage:** the dissenter may choose whether the dissent is:
  - signed
  - anonymized
  - attributed collectively if multiple panelists share it

## Authorship and contributor status
We explicitly state the following:
- Filing a formal dissent does **not** remove authorship eligibility.
- A contributor does **not** need to agree with every item to remain an author.
- To remain an author, a participant must judge the final output to be a fair representation of the process and results, even where they disagree with specific content.
- A participant may request:
  - authorship with dissent noted
  - acknowledgment rather than authorship
  - withdrawal from a specific output

## Appeals
An appeal may be made only on **procedural grounds**, not simply because a view did not prevail.

Appeals are limited to questions such as:
- the dissent was not properly recorded
- the wrong decision rule was applied
- the dissent was misrepresented in the final output

Appeals are submitted within **7 days**.

## Archiving and versioning
All formal dissents are archived in the project record.

For each dissent, the archive records:
- item number
- date submitted
- dissenter identity or anonymity status
- response taken
- whether the dissent was resolved, retained, or superseded in a later version

## Compact policy version
ECTO distinguishes between ordinary disagreement and formal dissent. Ordinary disagreement is addressed through revision, feedback, and rescoring across Delphi rounds. Formal dissent is a structured, persistent objection to a proposed item, section, recommendation, or framing that a participant wishes to have recorded in the project record and, where relevant, in public outputs.

A formal dissent may be submitted after round feedback is circulated and before the item is finalized. Dissents are concise, reasoned, and relevant to the item at issue. They may concern conceptual, methodological, pedagogical, scope-related, procedural, or ethical concerns. Dissents are screened only for procedural admissibility, not for substantive conformity with the majority view.

When a formal dissent is submitted, the coordination team may revise the item, split the item, carry the issue into a further round, or proceed while recording the disagreement as unresolved. If consensus is not reached, the item is marked accordingly and major reasons for disagreement are summarized. If consensus is reached but a formal dissent remains, the item may still be included, but the final output indicates that consensus was reached with recorded dissent.

Formal dissents do not automatically affect authorship or contributor status. Participants may remain authors while dissenting from specific items, provided they judge the final output to be an accurate representation of the process and results. All formal dissents are archived as part of the project record and may be revisited in future project updates.
